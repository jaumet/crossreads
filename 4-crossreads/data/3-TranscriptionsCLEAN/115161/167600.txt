I never heard if the cure was effectual, but the fellow never again reported to that quack, with pains in his back.  Yes! they have very quick methods of curing most ailments in the army.  I guess in civilian practice that same doctor would have taken longer to fix up the patient, especially if the patient appeared to be in affluent circumstances.
We were in Flers dug-outs for about a month, during which the camp was continuously shelled and shells were passing over us to other objects.
I was mighty glad when the time came for us to start our march "back" for a little "rest".
 
CHAPTER XVIII.
WE "GO OUT" FOR A REST 
It was about 24th. February that we left the dugouts and marched first to Perth camp, then the following day to Becourt camp, remaining there for a few days.
It was here that I was appointed a Lance Corporal, what for I can't say, but as it might get me out of some unpleasant fatigues, I was glad to receive the promotion.  I felt a man of some importance I can tell you, wearing a stripe.
We eventually arrive back at Buire, not a bad town, or perhaps I ought to call it a village.
I with about a dozen others are "billeted" in a large shed or kind of room adjoining a small house.  There is no door to this room, except through a room of the house, so as the house is "out of bounds", we have to climb through the an opening in the wall, which apparently was intended to be used as a window.
