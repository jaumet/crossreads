The sea is fairly smooth, which is fortunate &ndash; a sailor tells me during the choppy seas of the last few days the wounded suffered terribly when being put aboard the Hospital Ship.<br/>Even right out here an occasional shell comes buzzing throught the air and drops close alongside &ndash; it would be really rough luck to get hit so far away from the firing line after having been in such thick scrimmages.<br/>The ship I am on at present is the Sudan, a Castle Liner which is being used as a Naval Hospital Ship.</p>
<p>May 18<br/>Am transferred to the Galeka, another Castle Liner. This is not a proper hospital ship, there is only accommodation for 150 wounded &ndash; we have on board some 500 or 600, many very terrible cases, and the filth is awful. I request to be put on as orderly, at least I have the use of my limbs.</p>
<p>May  19<br/>Am on duty from 6 a.m. till 11 p.m. snatching foood when I can get it, which at any time I did not feel the need of.<br/>There is practically no nourishment for the patients; very little bread, the majority have to eat the hard ship's biscuits; jam, occasionally a little butter, very little milk &ndash; which is tinned- occasionally baron-tinned beef and a sort of cornflour.<br/>I have been watching two important brain cases &ndash; one man with a large portion of his brain exposed; the M.O.</p>
