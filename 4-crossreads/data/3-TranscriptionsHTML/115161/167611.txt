<p>&quot;painfully evident.&nbsp; They apparently had cannibalistic tendencies, as we soon discovered that the fair ones were quickly disappearing, while the darker &quot;chats&quot; increased greatly in numbers.&nbsp; I should imagine that the forebears of the &quot;dark-hued&quot; chat must have been closely related to the common house flea, as I fail to see how they could have travelled from one point of our bodies to another in such a short space of time without &quot;hopping&quot; at least a portion of the way.&nbsp; You would feel several bites in one locality of your anatomy and quickly &quot;view the situation&quot; to find that they had vacated that position, and were already no doubt &quot;forming up&quot; for an attack elsewhere.&nbsp; Yes! they were too lively for us.&nbsp; Fritz no doubt understood them.&nbsp; I wonder why they were so dark.</p>
<p>I hope you will forgive me for mentioning these vermin, but I really think that they are entitled to a little space, as they were without doubt one of the - if not the - greatest worries we had to contend with.</p>
<p>Just at the foot of the hill near my &quot;home&quot; was what remained of a huge sugar refinery.&nbsp; Many fellows camped among the ruins.</p>
<p>Our &quot;cookers&quot; were domiciled very close to these ruins.</p>
<p>Some fellows made dugouts along the sides of the roads, in the village, I did not quite fancy this idea, as running parallel with the main road was a battery of our big guns, and &quot;he&quot; was continually &quot;feeling&quot; for these guns with &quot;big stuff&quot; high explosive, and high velocity shells, to say nothing of the awful and paralising &quot;whiz-bang&quot;.&nbsp; The &quot;screaming&quot; of these shells as they passed over (perilously near at times) was most disconcerting.&nbsp; Night and day just the same, over they came.</p>
<p>Every night would see us on some fatigue work, carrying rations or bombs, or other stuff up to the front line, which was a very short distance away.</p>
