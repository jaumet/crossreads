Other teams would rush on  behind  &  by lucky chance may fight their way through. Thus the ceaseless procession went on.Rain cleared off on the night of the 26th  &  there was in consequence great aerial  &  artillery activity. However the wind changing again brought up cold   wind rain.Sunday 29th we received orders to move to the line  &  in consequence set off at 8.30. Rain still continued to fall  &  by the time we  reached Delville Wood  we were in a soaked condition. Here we bivoauced for a couple of hours building shelter  &  lighting fires. At about 4 P.M. we moved down the road to Fleurs passing en route numerous howitzer batteries on the edge of the wood. The road was heavy with mud  &  progress necessarily slow. Here  &  there broken or disabled transport wagons lay foundering in the slush by the road side, while in places mules  &  horses struggled in hidden craters.At dusk after an exhausting march we reached the sunken road near Fleurs. Passing into a communications trench it was soon evident that we were lost - as usual, so that a halt was called. Near by this place lay the ruins of one of the tanks - Having evidently been disabled
