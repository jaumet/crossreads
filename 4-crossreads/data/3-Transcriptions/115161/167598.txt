<p>While on fatigue work during the day we can see the Germans doing work behind their front line, so we are not very far away.</p>
<p>We had to shave every morning and the only way to procure water was by breaking through the ice in the shell holes. &nbsp;I don&#39;t think the razor had much to do with cutting the hair off, I really think it bent the bristles over and they snapped off, so perishing cold was it, standing outside our dug-out.</p>
<p>I had rather a funny - and what might have been &nbsp;a disastrous - experience one day. &nbsp;We were ordered to dig a trench just by the camp, and when I had dug down my section to a depth of about four feet, my spade struck something hard, on digging round very gingerly, I discovered that my spade had struck a large unexploded shell about an inch from the &quot;nose&quot;. &nbsp;I dug no more, but went straight away and reported the matter to the officer in charge of us, who had the shell removed by the engineers. &nbsp;They told me it was a very narrow escape for me and all the fellows in the vicinity. &nbsp;We came across a good many bodies, which did not look too pleasant, and&nbsp;certainly smelt&nbsp;none too wholesome. &nbsp;These things were all in a day&#39;s work, and did not effect us very much.</p>
<p>We were allowed a small piece of candle in our dug-outs, the result being that the heat from same would thaw the ice on the iron and make us comfortably wet.</p>
<p>Our battalion were in the front trenches for a couple of days, but nothing of an exciting nature took place.</p>
<p>I was sent back a few miles to &quot;Hobart&quot; camp, where there was a field hospital. &nbsp;My heels had become very bad again, and the doctor sent me &quot;back&quot;, so that a complete rest might cure them before marching started again.</p>
