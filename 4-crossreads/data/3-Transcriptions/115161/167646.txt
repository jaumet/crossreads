<p>After cleaning our rifles and ammunition we wander about the road until late in the afternoon, when an order comes round to &quot;fall in&quot;.&nbsp; My &quot;light-horse&quot; overcoat being too heavy to carry, on account of the quantity of mud thereto attached, I cut off all that portion &quot;south&quot; of the pockets.&nbsp; It looks very funny certainly, but it is much lighter.</p>
<p>We fall-in quickly as we have been told that we really are going out for a rest this time, and start marching along the road, but on discovering that the enemy are shelling it with remarkable exactitude, and after losing a few more out of our painfully thinned ranks, we go over the rough ploughed fields.&nbsp; The &quot;going&quot; is pretty heavy, but our hearts are light if our feet are heavy, and very few lag behind.</p>
<p>Judging by the amount of &quot;stuff&quot; that comes our way I should imagine &quot;he&quot; must be aware that we are the ones who &quot;beat&quot; him for the land we are now marching on.&nbsp; Anyway he is giving us a pretty solid farewell, and we appreciate it accordingly.</p>
<p>CHAPTER XX1X.</p>
<p><u>WE DO MOVE &quot;BACK&quot;, AT LAST</u>.</p>
<p>It is a good old saying that the third time is lucky.&nbsp; This applied to us on this occasion, we were in truth going out for a spell.&nbsp; What a poor old worn-out crowd we were, but managed to put some &quot;ginger&quot; in our step, due to the knowledge of our &quot;move&quot;, accentuated by the persistency of the enemy.&nbsp; We miss those who are not accompanying us, at the same time we could not help congratulating ourselves on getting through.</p>
<p>The pace is brisk until we are at a safe distance, then we sit down and have a well-earned smoke.</p>
