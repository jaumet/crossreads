<p>The distance was about fifteen miles, but had it been fifty it would have been just the same, we should have walked it.&nbsp; A more determined or stubborn fellow I never came across, but at the same time a better chap never walked.- (unless he had to)_ -<u>.</u>!!</p>
<p>On passing the guards which were placed on the frontier of France and Belgium we experienced no difficulty as the &quot;turned up hat&quot; was at once recognised and no questions asked.&nbsp; We were allowed to pass without being challenged.&nbsp; There were two Belgian and two French soldiers on guard at these points whose duty it was to see the passports of anyone passing over the frontiers.&nbsp; It was hardly likely that many would be going the way we were unless obliged to do so.&nbsp; It certainly would not be on a pleasure trip!!</p>
<p>We passed through Poperinghe on our way and stayed there and had a decent feed, and duly arrived at our destination early in the evening</p>
<p><u>KRUISSTRATT</u>.</p>
<p>Ye gods! what a place (if it ever had been one), nothing but ruins everywhere.&nbsp; Struck me at once as being a pretty hot place, and as things proved, I was not very far <s>from</s> wrong.</p>
<p>Adjoining Ypres, in fact a portion of the place, it seemed to be a kind of storage depot for shells and bombs after completing the job they had been sent over for by the enemy.&nbsp; I had heard bombs fall before this time, but for the consistency and painful regularity commend me to this place.&nbsp; Shells were continuous, and bombs at night, ceaseless.</p>
<p>We occupied a position on the main street, in a portion of a ruined house, the covering being just a sheet of galvanised iron.&nbsp; This was our office and sleeping quarters.&nbsp; All round were horse-lines, and the enemy was ever&nbsp;trying to reduce this means of transport, so that at night we had anything but a cheerful time.</p>
