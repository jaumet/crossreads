<p>3 January 1915<br/>Sunday</p>
<p>Reveille at 6.<br/>Stables as usual &amp; warned for Church after the usual line inspection.  Only 3 go however, Hal, Bert and Bill go on leave &amp; I have to fill in afternoon as best as I could, had an hours sleep and then wrote letters to Perl, mother &amp; Florie.  I also received a note from both Glady &amp; Dot making the first since I left Australia. The [indecipherable] nearly meets his doom for he was out riding with Bill goes to take a jump &amp; finds in time that on the other side is a hole thirty feet deep.  I seen a lovely bay window in a mansion here it was a column stiled &amp; [indecipherable] roof the sashes were divided down the century had the usual small panes &amp; the fanlights were reversed this had the effect of setting it off great.  The boys drive home at 10.30.  Bert has proved himself an interpreter.  Bed 11 pm.</p>
<p>4 January 1915<br/>Monday 11 wks from home</p>
<p>Reveille at 6<br/>On first parade I was warned to appear as a witness at the 3rd L H in connection with Teddy's assault case the case came up at nine at the chap got 14 days, we the went to HQ, to [indecipherable] up the Signal Register then after dinner we travelled down to Tura for the usual work Corp Latimer went into Cairo and met the Company's turn out &amp; got at last some of our spares &amp; shoes.  Stables at 5 and Parade at 6 Station work was the order Hal and [indecipherable] went up the town for a stroll instead I wrote to Florie and fixed up an acquaintance for Harold. Our troop from today contains a C O, a Bde Major &amp; Adjutant more Downing's fads you would think he was I C  of 3 to 400 troops his [indecipherable] are about twice as big as his [indecipherable]. Have applied for leave &amp; got it for once. Adjutant.</p>
