<p>You hear their weird song which sings them on their way, &amp; it becomes intensified as they approach nearer but you don't know where they are going to land.  It is impossible to locate them until they have struck, &amp; then up goes everything within their reach.  The concussion is frightfully &amp; powerfully intense &amp; you are lifted about as if you were a mere straw.  What puny mites we are &amp; how helpless one feels against these mighty engines of war.  "Dig" is Kitchener's advice, &amp; that is the only advice.  Hide yourself in the bowels of the earth.</p>
<p>Capt. C.W. Melvill, New Zealand Staff Corps, has arrived &amp; taken the place of Capt. Mair as GSO (3).  He tells many interesting &amp; amusing tales of his experiences in France 12 months ago in the battle of the Aisne &amp; other stupendous struggles in the early stages of the war.  He had his arms &amp; legs lacerated by H-E, &amp; had 4 months in hospital in England.</p>
<p>His recital of instances of the brutal callousness of German methods of warfare were to a degree more repulsive than I have read of despite the horribleness of some of the latter <strike>have been</strike>.</p>
<p>He tells us of German officers entering quiet homes of the villagers &amp; stabling their horses in the dining &amp; drawing rooms tying them up to pianos, bedsteads, etc., &amp; generally converting the houses into dung pits &amp; cesspits for their filthy excreta &ndash; being blackguardly plunder &amp; desecration of beautiful homes.</p>

<p>Friday 12th Nov 1915</p>
<p>Nothing eventful &ndash; few Turkish shells.</p>
<p>Saturday 13th Nov 1915</p>
<p>Lord Kitchener paid a short visit to Anzac where he was introduced to various Divisl. &amp; Bde Commdrs.  He was given a great cheer on his arrival.  It was a peremptory official visit and he was gone again after an inspection of the various posts.</p>
