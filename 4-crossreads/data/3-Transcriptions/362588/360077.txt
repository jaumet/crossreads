<p>Another most scandalous thing &ndash; and one of which I can speak first hand - <u>was</u> the treatment of the wounded;  (I say "<u>was</u>" because conditions are very much better now).  Of course no one expected perfect organisation on land &ndash; the nature of the country &amp; the operations prevented that.  The authorities must have known there were going to be heavy casualties, yet the preparations therefor were terrible.  To take my own case, I was put on a lighter at &frac14; to 10 p.m. on the Monday night together with some 40 others.  There was no water on the lighter &amp; we suffered a good deal from thirst.  We were over 6 hours going from ship to ship to get one to take us, but they were all full of wounded, until we were eventually put on a ship by slings.  Bumping up &amp; down against the ship's sides whilst the middy in charge implored the various ship's authorities to take us was not pleasant.</p>
<p>The A.M.C. doctors &amp; orderlies worked magnificently &ndash; I don't think they ever took their clothes off &ndash; they were simply splendid, but far too few:  four on my boat.  I did not get my wounds dressed till the Wednesday afternoon.  Bandages gave out, &amp; they had to tear up the ship's sheets.  22 poor chaps died at sea, but I do not know if any could have been saved.  They were not prepared for us at Alexandria.  I was very lucky in getting to a good Hospital.  Some poor devils were put into a building &amp; had to listen to the incessant hammering whilst they fitted the place up as a Hospital.</p>
