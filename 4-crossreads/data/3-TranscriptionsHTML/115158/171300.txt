<p>1915</p>
<p>about 12 noon, &amp; since 9 a.m. when the Germans had been sighted, the colliers had been cast off from the British ships, &amp; steam raised for full speed.&nbsp; Admiral&nbsp; Sturdee then sent out his smallest ships &quot;Glasgow&quot;, &quot;Cornwall&quot;, &quot;Kent&quot; &amp; &quot;Carnarvon&quot; to draw the enemy.&nbsp; The &quot;Inflexible&quot; &amp; &quot;Invincible&quot; then moved out.&nbsp; It came as an unpleasant surprise to the Germans to see tripod masts moving out of the harbour.&nbsp; This fact told them that they had come into contact with super-Dreadnoughts, so that the odds were hopelessly against them, both as regards speed &amp; gun power.&nbsp; He then decided to turn &amp; run, &amp; our ships gave chase.&nbsp; &quot;Inflexible&quot; &amp; &quot;Invincible&quot; took on &quot;Scharnhorst&quot; &amp; &quot;Gneisnau&quot;, &quot;Carnarvon&quot; was despatched to sink the two ships in Port Pleasant, &amp; the remainder went after the other German cruisers, with the result that all except &quot;Dresden&quot; were sunk.&nbsp; A few survivors were picked up from the &quot;Gneisnau&quot;.&nbsp; The action lasted nearly 5 hours.&nbsp; The &quot;Dresden&quot; escaped to the Southward.&nbsp; &quot;Invincible&quot; was holed by an 8&quot; shell from &quot;Scharnhorst&quot; through a bunker, making a hole 7 feet by 2 feet while another one wrecked the Wardroom.&nbsp; One of the 4&quot; guns in the forward battery was blown into 2 pieces by a German shell.&nbsp; &quot;Inflexible&quot; was nearly torpedoed by &quot;Gneisnau&quot;, being only narrowly missed.&nbsp; It was stated by survivors that in &quot;Gneisnau&quot; an Enginer Officer who was too free in the use of his revolver to coerce</p>
