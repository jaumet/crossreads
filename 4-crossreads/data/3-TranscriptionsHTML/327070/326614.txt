<p>When the men had all had their turn at the canteen we were taken to the sergeant's mess where drinks were served, &amp; at about 1.30 the men were formed up &amp; marched down to the wharf where, with a little trouble over some who would break back, most were got on board &amp; towed out to the "Osterley". 1 officer per unit had to go back, the remainder being allowed to remain on shore until the second batch arrived, which was to be about 2.15.</p>
<p>It was my good fortune to remain ashore, &amp; in this time I had a ride in a rickshaw up to the Post Office where I sent off the cable, &amp; then down to the native quarter for a hurried look round. I made very few purchases, prices being much higher than I thought reasonable, &amp; similar chances offering, than say, in Egypt.</p>
<p>Hurried down to the wharf to find no sign of the barges being ready to leave the ship &amp; that all the other officers but one had gone to one of the swell places for lunch. The 2 of us had an ice-cream, meantime being shown fans &amp; silk goods by pressing salesmen, &amp; then went back to find the barges approaching. Some of the officers did not turn up, &amp; when the artillery, light-horse, etc had disembarked, we got them in their units preparatory to marching off.</p>
<p>There was a lot of bungling about the moving off, no one knowing who was supposed to be in charge, &amp; it was with some difficulty that I prevented the body breaking away off the wharf &amp; scattering.
