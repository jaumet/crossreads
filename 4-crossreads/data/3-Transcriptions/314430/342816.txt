By this time, and since morning, we had marched some 35 kilos, and <strike>we</strike> were now called upon to scale a track up <strike>over</strike> a steep hill. On arrival at the top, another halt was made on the edge of a wood, and the conducting officer disappeared, he ordered us forward <strike>again</strike> once more. We struggled up, and <strike>were once more</strike> resumed the wearying <strike>tramp</strike> work. At length we struck a main road which led us into the village of Lahoussoye (Map Reference 62 NW, I 3, C 55) where we discovered Brigade Headquarters. The draft was then split up, the reinforcements for the 17th, 18th, 19th, and 20th* Battalions being dismissed to the <strike>bi</strike> billets assigned to each unit. <strike>We were allo</strike> The 20th Reinforcements were allotted a cow shed. We stacked our kits here, and then went <strike>up</strike> to the cookers for a Dixie of tea. It was now 6 P.M. The cookers were situated in an orchard on the <strike>other end</strike> outskirts of the village. As I made my way thither, I had my first experience of shell fire. In a wheat field, two hundred yards away, shells were falling, and splashing the earth in all directions; but I was too tired to be very much startled. I got a drop of tea and sitting on a broken box, drank it; meanwhile, I watched the shells falling</p>
<p>* These battalions formed the 5th Brigade of the 2nd Division of the A.I.F.</p>
