<p>1919<br/>Jan 16<br/>his boots &amp; landed at the Battn later in his socks.  After 3 hours we were all ready to go on again.  When the train was at last ready, it whistled furiously, &amp; soldiers could be seen running towards the train from all directions, some from about &frac12; kilo away.  All along the line for the next kilo or so men would hop in, &amp; the train was going at a smart pace (for a troop train) by the time the last man was aboard.  We had now reached a coal mining district, &amp; passed mines on either hand, all along the line.  We stopped at Manage &amp; then carried on to Charleroi which we reached about 10 p.m.  The journey had taken about 54 hours, the longest time I was ever in a train.  We marched off from the Railway station to the Corps rest billets, a large warehouse next to the station.  It had been used by Fritz &amp; was filled up with bunks. (Fritz always made good sleeping quarters.  In the morning we watched some Fritzies shovelling muck off the ground in front of the billets.  A lot of Belgians also had their noses glued up against the iron fence</p>
