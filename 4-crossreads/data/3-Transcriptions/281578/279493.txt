<p>He didn't roar, just said "I didn't send you souvenir hunting." Out of the line resting, is different to camp discipline, &amp; the officers &amp; men are more pally. We came back to our rest place from the wiring at 10 a.m. &amp; had a loaf all day. I got a huge mail in the afternoon, letters galore from home &amp; Elsie, &amp; Bon sent a crowd he had received. I also got letters No 31 &amp; 32 from Elsie, &amp; one from Miss Tofft in the front line trench last Friday night, today I got a parcel from Miss Tofft, some sweets, she's a good sort, there's no doubt, as I only met her in England. If our chaps aren't the limit! I saw a sight this afternoon that would be worth pounds in the illustrated papers; some of our coves whom I told about the piano, went into the town, found an old shell-torn cart, pulled it to the chateau, &amp; actually arrived at our dugout, pulling the cart with the piano on. They unloaded it, &amp; in no time one of them was playing the latest airs, &amp; the soft, lively music was better than the roar of shells. An English major went by &amp; asked them would they sell it, he said it was worth 2000 francs; had he offered them they'd have said "It's yours" pretty quickly.
