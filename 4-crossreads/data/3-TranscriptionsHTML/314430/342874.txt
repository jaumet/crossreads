On our right was a wood masking several French batteries of "Seventy fives". These barked continuously, and the German batteries were replying intermittently, most of the<strike>ir</strike> enemy fire being directed on a strangely-fashioned, red-brick house on the verge of the wood. This was called "the doll's house". We started to traverse the two thousand yards <strike>yards</strike> of open country, keeping to the road. On each side <strike>there</strike> were fields of wheat, but these, and the road, were broken by shell craters. On looking across this tortured area I saw what was left of Villiers-Bretonneaux. <strike>in the distance</strike> At first glimpse merely a jagged stone column &ndash; formerly the church tower, I believe - <strike>stands</strike> could be seen above clumps of trees. On drawing nearer, I could observe how the place had been smashed and ruined by the enemy's fire, and noted a line of fretted walls and broken buildings. At dusk we were within half a mile of the position we had to hold. Suddenly a plume of black smoke appeared above us; there was a "ping", and another "ping", and another "ping".
