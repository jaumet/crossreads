<p>As the names of those enlisting were published daily in the evening paper, I hastened to the office of the Daily News requesting them to keep my name out until the following evening&#39;s issue, so that I should have an opportunity of telling my people before they could see the paper. On arrival at the station on my way home I espied my elder daughter scanning the Daily News, but as she met me with &quot;Ah! you didn&#39;t get&nbsp;through then&quot;. I naturally concluded that my name did not appear. On my perusing the aforesaid paper I discovered my own name on the top of a new column, she having thought that the list ended at the bottom of the previous column. I was content, and already felt a hero!!</p>
<p>CHAPTER 3.</p>
<p><u>I REPORT FOR DUTY.</u></p>
<p>Between the date of my enlistment and going into camp, very little of any consequence took place, I received congratulations and the hero-worship, which had been the cause of so much envy to me. I eagerly scanned the newspapers to see if the end of the war was (in their opinion) approaching, but to my chagrin things appeared to be getting worse: and in due course the 3rd. April 1916 arrived.</p>
<p>I left home at 7.30 a.m. in a nice blue suit, white collar and cuffs, with a huge gladstone bag and a large rug, and reported at the Drill Hall, Perth at 9.a.m. prompt.</p>
<p>There were one&nbsp;hundred and thirty-nine others, who (apparently) also favored this day rather than the 1st.</p>
