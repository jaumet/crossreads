<p>Everything seemed so strange, it was so weird to be walking along roads, and seeing good camps and comfortable dug-outs, well timbered and safe, which had so lately been used and occupied by the enemy.&nbsp; It was very evident that what he had done he had done thoroughly.</p>
<p>When passing over cross-roads we would quicken our pace slightly, anticipating that at any moment the road might &quot;go up&quot;.&nbsp; We got through however, and after a few more miles arrived at a terribly knocked-about village.</p>
<p>Think I&#39;ll start another chapter as I have a good deal to say about this village of Vauxl.</p>
<p>CHAPTER XX.</p>
<p><u>OUR LIFE AT VAUXL</u>.</p>
<p>Vauxl! ye gods! shall I ever forget this place?</p>
<p>We arrived here late in the afternoon, so the main thing was to fix ourselves up with some abode for the night.</p>
<p>The snow had all melted, a thaw had set in, turning what had been hard ground into mud and slush.&nbsp; We had by now become quite used to this kind of thing, &#39;twas certainly not nearly as cold, but the wet seemed to get right into your bones.</p>
<p>My friend the corporal (with whom I had shared a dug-out at Flers) and a couple of others and myself, secured a good position, against a portion of a broken wall of a building, this bit of wall was all that remained of the building.&nbsp; We went round and collected as much iron as possible to enable us to make a bit of a shelter from the weather.&nbsp; Succeeded in making a kind of &quot;lean to&quot;, and put our blankets and other things we possessed underneath, and then went in search of our evening meal.</p>
