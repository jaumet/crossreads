<p>chap near me got a nice "Blighty" a hit in the shoulder, the stretcher bearers took him away, lucky dog. Had I been closer to where the bullet was fired from, it would have gone through the hat, but without it, as it was, I'd have been killed on the spot. Before I could get away with my lot, both sides started shelling ferociously. Fritz's shells crashed all round our trench, &amp; it was useless to attempt to set off to the rear with the party. The artillery (ours firing the most) played their duet for an hour, &amp; as Fritz seemed to be letting our trench alone for a while, I set off with my party of chaps, &amp; Americans. The Yanks were very windy. No wonder poor beggars, their first sight of war. We had to go away down to the chalkpit beside a road where the limbers could bring them so far, &amp; we had to carry them on. His shells were falling back here too. The rations hadn't arrived, so the 4 parties waited in the chalkpit, we were tired &amp; dozed off to sleep. Talk about the luck of the game, the shells were bursting up on top of each side of the hollow, &amp; had they dropped in, they'd have got lots. The limbers finally came up about 12, &amp; the lads lumped the petrol tins full of tea &amp; the big thermos of stew for the line.
