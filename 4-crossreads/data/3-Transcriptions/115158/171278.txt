<p>Dec. 11. &nbsp; &nbsp; It was now quite a month since we had heard any war news, &amp; we were anxious to hear how things were going. However, one of our colliers brought several English papers, &amp; a few American papers of a recent date. During our stay here one of the&nbsp;sailors of the Japanese ship &quot;Hison&quot; was accidently knocked into the sea &amp;&nbsp;drowned. His body was not recovered until the following day, when an impressive funeral was held. The corpse was placed under a canopy on the upper deck, &amp; surrounded with as many flowers &amp; green leaves as could be gathered on shore. Then the whole ship&#39;s company filed past &amp; each bared his head, bowed towards the corpse, &amp; and apparently uttered a short prayer. Then the body was landed for burial. On some days it would rain incessantly, the atmosphere becoming damp, heavy, &amp; oppressive, while the thickly wooded shore would actually give off clouds of steam, investing everything with an air of unhealthiness. There was a German settler living here, &amp; when we sent people on shore in search of food he replied that he would not supply food to &quot;English dogs and niggers&quot;. Presumably the Japanese were the niggers. Later he left for Panama probably to disclose our whereabouts, but this we did not fear, because&nbsp;we knew that there was no enemy within 2000 miles.
