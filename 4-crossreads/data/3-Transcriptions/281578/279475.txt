<p>in the line. I was with the party that went to B Coy. &amp; it is an eye-opener. They talk of "front line" of trenches, it was simply isolated holes &amp; dugouts, &amp; on a dark night one could easily walk right into the German lines. It was more like outposts than the front line. I found Boo while there, he was O.K. Unfortunately, our company have had 3 casualties; Cpl. Evans was hit by a shell while handing out rations, &amp; died later, Dibden &amp; Holmes, the latter Capt Wallach's batman, were wounded by shells when well back. It is a hard job to find your way at night, in fact the Germans seem to be right round in a circle, &amp; Lor' knows how a commander allots his front. Hot stew &amp; tea came up on the limbers at 8 p.m. in big thermos heaters &amp; petrol tins, my word it was acceptable. The dirt on this page is trench mud, &amp; the smudges are rain drops as I lay in this desolate pozzy writing. <br/>Tuesday 30 April At 3 a.m. we were on ration carrying again. The different parties went down the road to the Crucifix, where the limber brings the rations. I hate that spot, Fritz knows it, &amp; two shells burst with a roar right in our ears. We quickly grabbed a petrol tin of tea each &amp; the bacon, &amp; hurried away. It beat me how the mules stood there so quietly.
