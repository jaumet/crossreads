<p>When came the parting of the ways between Mr. Churchill and Lord<br/>Fisher? It is obvious that it was some time in the first half of<br/>March, when 'across the prospects of the operations a shadow began to<br/>pass'. It would seem as if Lord Fisher became sceptical of the whole<br/>enterprise directly he realised the inability of the Fleet to clear<br/>the enemy's mine field, or to locate any other underwater defences,<br/>the difficulties of silencing the forts of the Narrows by long range<br/>direct fire, and the inability of the Fleet to knock out the mobile<br/>batteries on both sides of the Straits. He evidently realised that<br/>none of the conditions precedent for a successful attempt to force<br/>the Narrows had been fulfilled, and under the circumstances the Fleet<br/>might be faced with a grave disaster.</p>
<p>But these obstacles, none of which had been overcome, seem to<br/>have had the reverse effect on Mr. Churchill, and to have nerved him<br/>on to a greater determination to rush the affair through, in spite of<br/>the existence of the unswept mine field, the unsilenced forts, the<br/>mobile batteries, and other even more deadly defences which may, or<br/>may not, have existed at this period.
