<p>A reply was received expressing regret &amp; condolences, but pointing out that the Convoy must not stop, although it could "slow down" during the Service.</p>
<p>At the proper time all three vessels approached quite close together.</p>
<p>Every man was paraded The Chaplain held a short service preached a suitable sermon, &amp; expressed sincere sympathy for the relatives of the deceased soldier who was a man well known to &amp; well liked by quite a number of officers &amp; men on board the vessel</p>
<p>After appropriate hymns were sung the slowing down in the speed of the vessel indicated that the time was approaching for the burial.</p>
<p>The "Startling" reports from the Rifles of the firing party rang out.</p>
<p>Amidst a very marked silence the mournful notes of the "Last Post" were blown by the bugler, &amp; with the "fading" away of the last sound, the body was consigned to the deep.</p>
<p>Regret was expressed on all sides that the death should have taken place when within a few days steam of Capetown.</p>
<p>However before the voyage was over four deaths occurred, the last one actually within sight of the distant shores of Freemantle.</p>
<p>It seemed so very hard that these men should have battled with the grim destroyer, only to be beaten when the long looked for haven was almost attained.</p>
<p>The funeral service seemed to cause the whole of the Ships Company to prefer spending that Sunday in the quietest manner possible.</p>
<p>The following morning quite a stir was created by news leaking out that a wireless had been received that special care would have to be exercised for an "Enemy Raider" was reported to be in the neighbourhood, a sailing vessel having recently been sunk.</p>
