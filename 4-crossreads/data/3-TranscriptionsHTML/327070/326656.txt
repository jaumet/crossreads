<p>When all the new guard reliefs had gone out &amp; the old sentries returned came further compliments after which the old guard marched off after the band. Altogether the performance was quite spectacular &amp; we both enjoyed it.</p>
<p>After morning tea, we decided that it would be a good opportunity of going up the Nile by native boat ("veluka" so called) to a place called Barrage where some of the highest class live, a place noted for its beautiful gardens &amp; scenery, so "garry'd" back to Cairo to find out from Thos Cook &amp; Son what the fare should be if we engaged on to take us up, our idea being to return by train.</p>
<p>20 piastres (abt 4/3) they said at most. We took tram down to Boulak Bridge &amp; hunted about for a waterman. After crossing the bridge to Gezireh Is we found a merry-faced young man who owned a boat &amp; at first asked 50 P.T. for the trip. Offered 25, but he wanted 6/- (30 P.T.) &amp; after a lot of haggling we left him to try &amp; find some other boat owner.</p>
<p>The road we followed led along the bank of the Nile (abt &frac14; ml wide here) thro' the well-kept gardens of Gezireh. Moored along the bank were scores of house-boats, regularly occupied by French &amp; Egyptian people, some of them being quite large &amp; containing perhaps 100 to 200 apartments. Across the Nile were the buildings of Cairo with a clump of date palms here &amp; there : house  boats &amp; dhows of varying size were moored to the opposite bank.</p>
