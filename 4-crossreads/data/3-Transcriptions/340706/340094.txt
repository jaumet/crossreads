<p>140<br/>have lost their rifles &ndash; their equipment &amp; now stand with new uniforms &amp; a kit bag. All that marching is over &amp; here are lorries which could never be found in the days of the war.</p>
<p>At 4.30 we arrive at Prince George a cross channel steamer built for 25 but only capable of doing 18 &amp; on this trip 14 knots &ndash; We pass out through breakwater &amp; look back at Havre glorified in a fading sun which sets tonight at 8.30 pm.</p>
<p>I cannot feel that I am leaving France for good but that I shall see it again though perhaps not Le Havre. In this frame of mind I settle down to dinner &amp; do not watch the shore fade away. The sea is like glass not a wave upon it only a ripple yet many continue to get sea sick I dont know how but they did. Sunset is a failure as there is too much low lying mists</p>
<p>I am on duty on deck till 1 am &amp; then when anchor is dropped turn in. We have anchored at the mouth of river having passed Isle of Wight when passing up the [indecipherable] &ndash; Though night is clear all that one can see is a succession of lights &amp; lighthouses. The old Boom looms up or rather the connecting Forts.</p>
