<p>Being the first we had up till then seen, we could not but admire their grit &amp; pluck &amp; did not forget to tell them so. They did their work splendidly. It seemed so strange to see quite a young girl arranging, at the train, for the motor cars to take their loads of wounded &amp; the organization was very good.</p>
<p>On arrival at the Hospital each man was taken in charge of, by the sister in charge of the ward &amp; 2 orderlies, &amp; after all his particulars battalion, wounds, etc, had been taken, for the first time for about six months he was gently placed between clean sheets white as snow.</p>
<p>What a sensation it caused! However almost before the first beautiful thoughts had passed, sleep, glorious sleep, soon changed a bustling ward into a quiet calm.</p>
<p>How can one describe the feelings on waking next morning it is almost certain that quite a number, on the instant of waking that morning really thought they were in Heaven itself, for even had they been, their feelings could not have been more pleasant. Heave</p>
<p>Transferred from all the din, &amp; roar, the mud &amp; water, blood stains of the wounded, the groans of the dying, the pain &amp; the weariness, to a quiet, lovely bed with spotless white sheets, with Sisters &amp; Nurses with kindly faces, as it were, quietly "hovering" about was deliciously restful. It was therefore not surprising that one did say He thought he was in Paradise &amp; if it was not, well he for one was satisfied with this!</p>
<p>Lying in a ward in the Hospital, &ndash; this particular Hospital No 30 General at Calais was composed of large Marquees, house accommodation being unavailable, &ndash; the ordinary Military routine more or less continues here, &amp; being a "Tommy" Hospital, naturally the military "Red Tape" dicipline is more forcibly applied.
