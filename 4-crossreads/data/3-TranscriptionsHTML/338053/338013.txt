<p>Herrod steadied them &amp; got them to go back into position. About 6 o'clock the fire was very hot &amp; one shell landed on the section of our trench on the left killing Capt. Cotton &amp; a sergeant &amp; burying several others. We daren't let the boys know that night that Cotton had gone, &amp; not knowing how the 8th Bn. line was in front of us had a most anxious night hourly expecting a counter attack to be launched against us. It did not come, but we lost our sleep nevertheless.<br/>Tuesday July 26th proved another fine day. Early in the morning Fritz opened up again. In my tiny diary I see I made the entry "Hell, but yet heaven". The boys were holding out splendidly &amp; Capt. Herrod was simply magnificent but the anxiety for the boys was almost too great. It was absolutely impossible to do anything in the way of replying to the German fire : one could only crouch &amp; be pounded at all the time wondering whether the shrill scream heralded the shell that was going</p>
