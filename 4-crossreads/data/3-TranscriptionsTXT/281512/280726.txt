 on through the old Zonnebeke Station (now absolutely swept away) up to Broodsende &amp; entered the railway Cutting near the ridge crest. Shells began to fall just about a hundred paces ahead &amp; their skyrocket like whizz, without cessation, passing too close overhead &amp;  bursting all around, induced us to retire. The light too failed &amp; rain set in. We got no pictures but whips of fun. I felt great admiration for the stretcher bearers, who slowly plodded on with their burdens, trudging through mud &amp; presenting a tempting target, for the enemy observation balloons had eyes on everything. It was impossible to bring in many wounded under these conditions, &amp; many poor devils must perish from exposure. I noticed one awful sight: a party of, ten or so, telephone men all blown to bits.
