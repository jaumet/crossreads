I arrived at the Post, which we are attached to, about 8 a.m. on the 16th inst. &amp; things were fairly busy for a while,  a great number of our stretcher cases were gas patients,  Fritz put plenty of gas shells over during the night &amp; the ambulance we relieved had to send quite a number of their bearers to the Hospital. In fact a few gas shells landed in the vicinity where we were working. On the way to the Post I met Max Allworth,  he told me that he saw Oscar Hann only 2 days ago. Also met Capt. Wilkinson from the Rifle Club.  Plenty of mud about but unfortunately the weather is clearing up &amp; the track should be soon O.K.</p>
<p>17th October 17.<br/>A quiet night, only a couple of casualties occurring in our vicinity. In the morning the ground was dry &amp; we were able to put our patients on trucks &amp; push them along the rails to the next Relay Station,  a great assistance. Lieut. Gillies from Neutral Bay called at the dugout to see me.  I was naturally very much surprised to see him.</p>
<p>18th October 17.<br/>Shelling rather severe for a couple of hours during the night,  we were obliged to take shelter in a "Pill Box"    A number of troops were hit in our vicinity to naturally plenty of work. Some of the incidents that occurred fairly shook us up,  fancy calling this a civilised world &amp; this ghastly business going on. The next 48 hours we did at the furthest post but we were indeed very fortunate, only having 5 carries amongst 4 squads,  but even when we have very few carries it is a great strain on a chap living amongst the desolate scene &amp; continual shelling. Just received the news that Lieut. Gillies was killed this morning.</p>
<p>20th October 17.<br/>Hurrah, our relief arrived at 8 a.m. so we soon made ourselves scarce I can assure you. It was a long &amp; dreary walk back to the Dressing Station, fully 4 miles away.
