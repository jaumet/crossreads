<p>on our left &amp; ahead, a quarter mile off lies the town of Suez, its buildings resting low upon the water.  The port is full of shipping &amp; everything around us is life.</p>
<p>This contact with civilization has put good humour into the Troops.  In full service dress they crowd the rails &amp; bargain for fruit, sweetmeats &amp; P'Cs with the miscellaneous crowd of Arabs &amp; other dusky skinned desert sons who have tied their boats to the ship's sides.  I have fed on Barbary grapes &amp; Turkish Delight &amp; bought Post Cards of Port Said to send home.</p>
<p>How happy &amp; contented I feel.  My whole soul harmonises with the perfect serenity of the scene around me.</p>
<p>What a picture for an artist to conceive, or put to canvas.  Not a wisp of cloud mars the blue dome overhead, a soft zephyr plays &amp; gambols around us just sufficient to temper the hot sun's rays &amp; cause a dancing ripple upon the Sea. &amp; Near us the water sparkles in light blue and as the eye travels towards the shore becomes darker &amp; finally ends near the golden sands in an infinite royal blue.  My opinion of "far fetched" paintings has changed.  I have looked upon a picture more dazzling in its varied hues than any master painter could conceive.</p>
<p>As noon approached the cool wind of the morning was consumed by a boiling sun &amp; from then till sunset the heat was unbearable.  Evening however brought the pleasant conditions of the early morn.</p>
<p>An Indian troopship "Zeesta" arrived during afternoon with Gurkhas &amp; Sikhs on board for Marseilles.  Our officers entertained their officers at dinner on "Berrima".</p>
<p>Monday 26th July 1915</p>
