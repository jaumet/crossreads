<p>About 8 o'clock we were going through the straits, the lights of Perim showing clearly on the starboard bow: in the daytime land is also visible on the opposite side, but we could see no sign of it. Four vessels passed us, 2 of them being quite close &amp; calling forth great cheering &amp; cooeeing from the men. A third was a long way off &amp; the fourth was a British cruiser said to be the "Minerva"(?) which is detailed to patrol these waters. I saw her flashlight going &amp; she asked us our name, refusing to reply to our query as to who she was. Afterwards I learned that a warship at night can always be distinguished by the fact that her head-lights are immediately above her side-lights.</p>
<p>A glorious night, Venus &amp; Jupiter being especially bright, &amp; the moonlight shimmer on the waves very beautiful. Phosphorescent patches still abundant.</p>
<p>Ship's run to noon 388 miles.</p>
<p>Thursday 10 February 1916</p>
<p>A clear sunny day, the heat of the sun being tempered by a cool N breeze. A fair roll during the morning but very little motion later in the day.</p>
<p>When we woke a small island with a lighthouse on it was in sight a couple of miles to the N, &amp; the sentry told me that we had passed 2 other island lighthouses during the early hours of the morning. About 8 o'clock we were abreast of another small island</p>
