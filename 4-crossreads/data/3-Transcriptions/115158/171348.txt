<p>1916</p>
<p>below the water line the &quot;New Zealands&quot; port outer propeller had pierced the wing compartments with a hole about two feet in diameter.&nbsp; Both port propellers were badly damaged on coming into dock, due to an awkward cross wind causing our stern to drift on to the dock side when entering.&nbsp; These defects, it was anticipated, would take about 2 months to make good in the floating dock, and Admiralty officials decided that the work could be done more expeditiously in a Royal Dockyard.&nbsp; Acrordingly, the Admiralty ordered the ship to be made temporarily seaworthy.&nbsp; When this had been done, and we had shipped two new propellers from &quot;Indefatigable&quot; &amp; &quot;Invincible&quot;, we were floated out of Dock, &amp; sent on our way to Devonport.</p>
<p>May. 1.</p>
<p>To avoid any rough weather it was deemed advisable to send us down the East coast, although this route was strewn with danger for us in the shape of mines &amp; torpedoes from submarines.&nbsp;</p>
<p>May 2.</p>
<p>However, we anchored in the Humber for the first night, &amp; the following night we anchored at the Nore.&nbsp; No traffic was allowed down the East coast at night, owing to the difficulty of avoiding our own mined areas.</p>
