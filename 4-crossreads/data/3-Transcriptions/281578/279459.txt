<p>put any on our cosy dug out. I went a couple of hundred yards to where B Coy. are in a village, &amp; showed Boo the home letter. My section of 5 are in a dug out with the stretcher bearers. They made a fire in an oil drum (Australians are great on a fire) &amp; yarned about their war experiences &ndash; it is easy to see what grand chaps there are over here, &amp; it is awful beyond words that so many fine men have been sacrificed. Had a good sleep in my clothes, blanket, &amp; overcoat, but the roar of guns woke me up about 12 &ndash; it always turns very cold towards morning. Sunday 21 April At 7 a.m. 13 platoon set off with picks &amp; shovels about a mile, digging new trenches. It was heavy walking across the mud of ploughed fields, &amp; heavier still digging in the sticky stuff. I'm glad I'm a N.C.O. &amp; don't have to do this hard fatigue, though I did a bit of shovelling. There was a white frost on the grass this morning, &amp; the day was bright &amp; sunny. An aeroplane of Fritz's was up a great height, &amp; our anti-aircraft guns were spreading white puffs of bursting shrapnel in the air near him, but didn't have the luck to bring him down.
