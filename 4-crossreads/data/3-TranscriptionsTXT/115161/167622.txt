 &quot;ADVANCE&quot;, - we were at once electrified into action.&nbsp; We sprang to our feet, and at first walked, gradually increasing our pace to a &quot;double&quot;&nbsp; We had not gone very far when to our surprise and alarm a further order was shouted out, RETIRE! 
 Retire! what did it mean?&nbsp; Had we failed?&nbsp; Were we being driven back instead of going forward?&nbsp; No! it is only that we have either started a moment or two too soon or advanced too quickly. 
 We lie down again and wait a few seconds only, then up again and forward, but already the lines have started to thin, a few have fallen.&nbsp; The machine-gun fire that we meet is terrible.&nbsp; Comrades are falling fast all round us, I stop to see if I can help one, but he is already beyond my help. 
 We stop and lie down for about a minute, while the barrage moves forward the prescribed twenty-five yards, then up again.&nbsp; I feel a sharp sting on the top lip.&nbsp; I am hit.&nbsp; I rush madly on with my head as far into my steel helmet as possible.&nbsp; (How absurdly small the helmet seems now, but how ridiculously large and clumsy they appeared when we first received them.) 
 I hear a voice shouting &quot;Gill! you&#39;re on fire&quot;.&nbsp; I look down and to my indescribable horror I discover that a bullet has exploded the cartridges in the bandolier, setting fire to my tunic on the left side.&nbsp; My alarm was intensified by the knowledge that in the pocket of the tunic were bombs.&nbsp; As both my hands were engaged, I simply fell down and rolled in the mud, and thus extinguished the fire.&nbsp; It gave me a very unpleasant feeling through.&nbsp; (On looking at the bandolier later I found that five of the cartridges had been practically cut through by a bullet passing through them.&nbsp; A pretty near go for me.&nbsp; I still have the remains of the bandolier and cartridges).
